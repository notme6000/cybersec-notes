## What is a Social Engineering Attack?

A **social engineering attack** is a type of cyberattack where the attacker **manipulates people** into giving confidential information, access, or money — instead of hacking systems directly.

It targets **human psychology** (trust, fear, urgency, curiosity).

---

# Types of Social Engineering Attacks (with examples)

 

## 1. Phishing

**What it is:** Fake messages pretending to be from trusted sources.

**Example:**
You receive an email from “Your Bank” saying:

> “Your account will be suspended. Click here to verify.”

You click and enter your password — attacker steals it.

 

## 2. Pretexting

**What it is:** Attacker creates a fake story (pretext) to gain trust.

**Example:**
Someone calls pretending to be IT support and says:

> “We are fixing a server issue. Please share your login to test.”

Victim gives credentials.

 

## 3. Baiting

**What it is:** Offering something tempting to trick victims.

**Example:**
A USB drive labeled “Salary Data 2026” is left in an office.
An employee plugs it in → malware installs automatically.

 

## 4. Tailgating (Piggybacking)

**What it is:** Physically following someone into a restricted area.

**Example:**
Attacker says, “I forgot my access card,” and an employee holds the door open.

 

## 5. Scareware

**What it is:** Fake warning messages to scare victims.

**Example:**
Popup says:

> “Your computer is infected! Click here to fix now!”

Victim installs fake antivirus (which is malware).

# Types of Phishing Attacks (with examples)

Phishing is the most common social engineering attack. Here are its types:

 

## 1. Email Phishing

**Fake emails sent to many people.**

**Example:**
Email from “Netflix” saying your payment failed.
You click link → fake website steals your card info.

 

## 2. Spear Phishing

**Targeted phishing for a specific person or company.**

**Example:**
An employee receives email appearing from their boss asking for confidential files.

 

## 3. Whaling

**Targets high-level executives (CEO, CFO).**

**Example:**
Fake legal notice email sent to a CEO asking for urgent wire transfer.

 

## 4. Smishing (SMS Phishing)

**Phishing via text message.**

**Example:**
SMS says:

> “Your package delivery failed. Click here to reschedule.”

Link leads to fake site.

 

## 5. Vishing (Voice Phishing)

**Phishing via phone calls.**

**Example:**
Caller pretends to be from the bank and asks for OTP or card details.

 

## 6. Clone Phishing

**Attacker copies a real email but replaces the link or attachment.**

**Example:**
You previously received a real invoice.
Attacker resends similar email with a malicious attachment.

## How to Protect Yourself

* Never share passwords or OTP
* Verify sender email carefully
* Don’t click suspicious links
* Enable 2FA
* Think before reacting to urgent messages
